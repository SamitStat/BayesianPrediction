1The goal, and the log-scale rulesetup

We want a number λ for each parameter–mechanism pair: how much mechanism k moves parameter p. There are 40 in all (10 parameters × 4 mechanisms) — the whole loading matrix Λ.

What is "the parameter"? Each of the ten is a knob in the SAWS rule that sets the shape of the force–displacement loop: F0/F1 are backbone forces, S0 a stiffness, du a displacement, r1–r4 the slopes of its branches, alpha/beta the pinching and degradation. A loading says how a mechanism nudges one of those knobs.

The model works on a log scale, so a loading does not add to the parameter — it multiplies it by eλ.

Why a log scale? These parameters are positive and act by proportion — "30% stiffer" means the same whether the base is large or small. Taking logs turns "multiply" into "add", so effects become additive and symmetric: a halving (×0.5) and a doubling (×2) sit the same distance from 0 (−0.69 and +0.69). That symmetry is exactly why a plain bell centered at 0 is a fair prior.

Example. λ = 0 means ×1 (no change). λ = +0.214 (capacity→F1) means ×1.24. λ = −0.197 (corroded→F1) means ×0.82 (an 18% drop). λ = +0.821 (corroded→r2) means ×2.34. Drag to feel the rule.

2What a prior is, and why it sits at 0chosen

A prior is your belief about a loading before looking at the data, drawn as a curve over its possible values. Tall = more believable.

Reading the curve. The whole area under it is your total belief (it sums to 1); the relative heights are relative plausibility. A tall, narrow curve says "I am fairly sure it is near here"; a low, wide one says "it could be many things."

Why have one? 40 unknowns from only 24 cells, each an average of 2 tests. The data alone would read random scatter as real effects. The prior says "most effects are modest; believe a big one only on strong evidence."

Why not let the data speak alone? The data enter through a second curve, the likelihood (how well each value explains the 24 cells), and Bayes' rule multiplies the two. With so little data the likelihood is broad for most loadings, so the prior keeps the answer sensible — and it steps aside wherever the data are sharp. Why center at 0? "No effect" is the honest default (×1). Why symmetric? A mechanism could push either way (corrosion lowers F1 but raises r2), so we do not pre-judge direction, only size. Why a bell? "Near 0 likely, far unlikely, symmetric" is exactly its shape.

Example. Centering at +0.5 would assume, before any test, a +65% effect (e0.5 ≈ 1.65) — assuming the conclusion. Drag the center; we keep it at 0.

3Spread: the one knobchosen

A bell at 0 has one knob: the spread σ — how far values typically sit from 0. Read on the real scale: σ = 0.3 calls a typical effect about e0.3 ≈ ×1.35; σ = 1 would call ×2.7 ordinary.

The 68 / 95 reading. For a normal, about 68% of the belief sits within ±σ of the center and about 95% within ±2σ. So σ = 0.3 says: two-thirds of the time the effect is within ±0.3 (about ×0.74 to ×1.35), and you would be quite surprised past ±0.6 (about ×0.55 to ×1.8).

Why one knob is enough. A normal is completely described by just two numbers, its center and its spread. We have fixed the center at 0, so the spread is the only thing left to choose — which is what the next sections do.

Example. A 50% effect (λ ≈ 0.4) is surprising at σ = 0.3 (past one spread) but unremarkable at σ = 1. Drag σ.

4One shared spread per mechanismchosen

A mechanism's ten loadings are the same kind of quantity, so they share one size τ for the whole family — and the data set it, we do not fix it. Use the buttons above to switch mechanism; each has its own τ. Drag τ: all ten move together. Small τ pulls the family to 0 (the mechanism switches off); large τ lets them grow.

Three options, and why this one. No pooling = give every loading its own free spread (it over-fits noise). Full pooling = force all loadings to one common value (it hides real differences). Partial pooling = each mechanism's ten loadings share one learned spread τ — the middle path that borrows strength without erasing differences.

How borrowing works. A loading the data measure well (corroded r2) pushes τ up; a higher τ then widens the believable range for that mechanism's poorly-measured loadings, so each is judged against its own family rather than against zero. If a family's measured loadings are all small, τ collapses and drags the whole family toward 0. Because τ is learned per mechanism, the data effectively decide whether a mechanism matters at all.

Example from your fit. Corrosion's family size is τ ≈ 0.30 (led by r2). Aging's settled near 0.11 and the aged×corroded interaction's near 0.02 — nearly switched off, which is exactly why aging and synergy come out null. Capacity's is ≈ 0.11, concentrated in F0/F1/S0.

5The one real derivation (and why this form)derived

The only real math: a standard bell (spread 1, draw z) multiplied by a fixed τ becomes a bell of spread τ. So "λ ~ Normal(0, τ)" and "λ = τ·z, z ~ Normal(0,1)" are the same statement. We write the second — a re-write, not a new law.

The algebra, slowly. If you multiply a random quantity by a constant τ, its spread multiplies by τ too (its variance by τ²). Start with z, spread 1; then τ·z has spread τ. Both z and τ·z are bells centered at 0, and a bell is completely fixed by its center and spread — so "spread-τ bell" and "τ times a spread-1 bell" are literally the same object.

Example. For corroded→r2: τ = 0.30, shape z = 2.7, so λ = 0.30 × 2.7 = 0.82 — the fitted value. Raise τ to 0.60 and, same shape, λ = 1.62.

Second reason for the product: it keeps the fitting program well-behaved. Picture the allowed loadings (up–down) against τ (left–right): in the direct form the allowed band pinches to a point as τ→0, a narrow neck a step-walking sampler cannot turn inside, so it stalls. As τ·z, the walker explores z on flat ground (always spread 1) and only afterwards multiplies by τ. The standard name for this trick is the non-centered parameterization; it is the funnel your Chapter 4 handled in the K=2 model.

The top curve below (z) never changes; drag τ and only the bottom (τ·z) stretches.

6z is the pattern, not the sizechosen

Because z has spread exactly 1, it carries only the pattern across the ten parameters — which are pushed, and which way — never the overall size, which is entirely in τ. The pattern below is the real fitted shape of the selected mechanism (each z = its loading ÷ τ).

Why separating size and pattern matters. If size and pattern were tangled in one set of numbers, you could double one and halve the other and get the same loadings — the model could not tell them apart. Fixing z to spread 1 removes that ambiguity: τ owns all the size, z owns only the shape.

Reading the signs. A positive z means the mechanism raises that parameter; negative means it lowers it. For corrosion the pattern reads "r2 up hard, F1 down, the rest small" — the physical story of damage moving into the post-peak branch.

Example. Drag τ to resize the whole shape; drag r2's z to move only the coral point. Switch to aged and the pattern is flatter (its biggest is r2 at z ≈ 1.7, but its τ is small, so the loading stays modest).

7A prior for τ: the half-normalchosen

τ is a size, so it cannot be negative, and small sizes are more believable. The half-normal is the simplest positive-only "small more likely" curve — an ordinary bell folded at 0.

Why a half-normal and not something heavier. A half-Cauchy would also be positive-only but allows much larger τ far more often; the half-normal is gentler and lighter-tailed — firm regularization that still lets τ grow when the data insist. It is the standard "weakly-informative" choice for a hierarchical scale.

A small subtlety. The "0.3" is the half-normal's scale, not the standard deviation of τ itself. What it pins down cleanly is the average of τ² (it equals 0.3²), which is exactly the quantity the next section needs. And the peak at 0 matters: because the most believable τ is 0, the prior actively permits a mechanism to be off until the data argue otherwise — the engine behind the aging and interaction nulls.

Example. HalfNormal(0.3) puts about 68% of its belief on τ below 0.3 (average ≈ 0.24). Every mechanism's τ is drawn from this same prior; the data then raised corrosion's to ~0.30 and left the interaction's near 0.02. Drag the scale; it never enters the shaded, not-allowed side.

8The number 0.3, chosen then checkedchosen

The 0.3 in HalfNormal(0.3) is picked by hand. On the log scale ±0.3 is about ±35%.

What "weakly informative" means. Strong enough to rule out absurd effects (×10, ×100) and to pull 40 noisy candidates toward 0; weak enough that real evidence can move any single loading far past it. 0.3 threads that needle.

The check, step by step (not a derivation of 0.3). A loading's variance is the average of (τ·z)². Since τ and z are independent, that is (average of τ²) × (average of z²). A half-normal of scale 0.3 has average τ² = 0.3² = 0.09; a standard normal has average z² = 1. So variance = 0.09 and spread = √0.09 = 0.3 — exactly the number we set, confirmed by the math, not produced by it.

Beatable, in numbers. At scale 0.3, the fitted corroded r2 (0.82) is about 2.7 spreads from 0 — far, but reachable, and the data took it there. At 0.05 it would be ~16 spreads away (the prior would veto it); at 2.0 even random scatter would routinely clear the bar. Drag the scale and compare the typical band to the coral 0.82 mark.

The complete loading matrix Λ (10 parameters × 4 mechanisms), all real fitted means.

How to read the grid. Rows are the ten parameters, columns the four mechanisms; color shows sign and strength (teal raises, blue lowers, stronger color = larger), and a coral outline marks a credible effect. These are contrasts (effect-coded ±½), so "corroded" is the full corroded-vs-not difference at average aging.

The whole story. Capacity credibly raises F0, F1, S0 (the code anchor — bigger nails / denser species are stronger). Corroded drops F1 ~18% and more than doubles the post-peak slope r2 (×2.34) — damage concentrated in the post-peak branch. Aged: all ten undecided. Aged×corroded: all ten undecided — no resolvable synergy. The two null families are exactly the ones whose shared τ the data pulled toward 0.

One caveat, kept honest. The capacity column is anchor-interpreted: capacity is a fixed function of species and size in this design (an aliasing limit), so read its column as "tracking the code-predicted strength," not as a freely estimated effect.
